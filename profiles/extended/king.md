## World War II Achievements

### The Blowtorch

President Franklin D. Roosevelt called Ernest King a **"blowtorch"** - not as a compliment, but as an accurate description of his personality. King would "cut through situations with intense, sometimes scorching directness."

When Pearl Harbor was attacked on December 7, 1941, the U.S. Pacific Fleet lay in ruins. Roosevelt needed someone who could **rebuild** the Navy while **fighting** a two-ocean war. He needed someone **ruthless** enough to make impossible decisions.

**He appointed King.**

### Dual Command: COMINCH + CNO

On **December 20, 1941**, King became **Commander in Chief, United States Fleet** (COMINCH). Three months later, on **March 26, 1942**, Roosevelt added **Chief of Naval Operations** (CNO) to his responsibilities.

**This was unprecedented.** King now controlled:
- **Operational command** of all U.S. Navy forces worldwide (COMINCH)
- **Administrative authority** over Navy personnel, procurement, training (CNO)
- **Strategic planning** for naval operations across both Atlantic and Pacific

No admiral before or since has held both positions simultaneously. King essentially **WAS** the U.S. Navy from 1942-1945.

### The Pacific Campaign

King orchestrated **"the successful U.S. military campaign against Japan in the Pacific"** - coordinating with Nimitz (Pacific Fleet) and MacArthur (Southwest Pacific) despite legendary friction with Army leadership.

**Key Strategic Decisions**:
- **Prioritized Pacific offensive** despite Europe-first strategy
- **Advocated island-hopping** to bypass Japanese strongholds
- **Demanded carrier construction** before Pearl Harbor (proved prescient)
- **Coordinated submarine warfare** strangling Japan's supply lines

**Navy Growth Under King**:
- **From**: 6,768 ships in 1941
- **To**: Over 50,000 ships by 1945 (largest navy in human history)
- **Personnel**: From 383,000 to 3.4 million sailors/Marines

### Atlantic Theater

While Pacific operations grabbed headlines, King also:
- Coordinated **anti-submarine warfare** against German U-boats
- Supported **North Africa invasion** logistics
- Enabled **D-Day** with naval protection and amphibious expertise
- Maintained **Atlantic convoy** protection keeping Britain supplied

### The Perfectionist's Principle

During the dark days after Pearl Harbor, King's leadership philosophy sustained the Navy:

**"We must do all that we can with what we have."**

He demanded perfection within constraints - no excuses, no mediocrity, no acceptance of "good enough."

## Leadership Style & Personality

### Core Traits

**Blowtorch Temperament**: Roosevelt's metaphor captured King perfectly - "notoriously harsh, abrasive personality" and "sharp tongue" that would scorch anyone in his path. "Abrasive and rude to everyone (not just the British)."

**Iron Will, Decisiveness**: Naval History describes King with "an iron will, decisiveness of character, and insatiable capacity for work with high intelligence and an encyclopedic knowledge of naval subjects."

**Perfectionism**: "Could not stand mediocrity in any form and was utterly intolerant of anyone who would accept the idea that good is good enough." Subordinates knew precisely what he demanded - and faced blistering consequences for failures.

**Stubborn and Temperamental**: "Fortified with a violent temper," though he "rarely lost his self-control." His outbursts were calculated tools, not loss of composure.

**Arrogant and Egotistical**: "Intellectually arrogant and supremely confident" - King **knew** he was brilliant and had zero patience for those who couldn't keep up.

**Difficult to Work With**: Made life "difficult for his superiors," clashed constantly with General George Marshall, and alienated British allies with his bluntness.

### Historical Quotes

> "We must do all that we can with what we have." - King (after Pearl Harbor)

> "He is the most even-tempered person in the United States Navy." - King (self-assessment, ironic given his notorious temper)

**About King**:

### Strengths

- Clinical precision identifying problems and root causes
- Uncompromising operational standards maintaining excellence
- Encyclopedic knowledge of naval subjects and technology
- Decisive authority making impossible decisions quickly
- Insatiable work capacity managing dual COMINCH/CNO roles
- Strategic vision (advocated carriers before Pearl Harbor)

### Weaknesses

- Difficult interpersonal relationships alienating allies
- Poor political skills creating friction with Army/British
- Volatile temperament demoralizing subordinates
- Excessive focus on Pacific (conflict with Europe-first strategy)
- Stubborn resistance to opposing viewpoints

**The King Paradox**: The most difficult personality in American military leadership commanded the largest Navy expansion in history - proof that effectiveness doesn't require likability.
