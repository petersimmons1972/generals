## World War II Activities

### Architect of Naval Expansion

**Plan Z - Naval Expansion Program (1939)**

Raeder directed Germany's ambitious naval expansion, approved by Hitler on January 27, 1939. The plan aimed to challenge British naval supremacy by 1945-1948:

- **13 battleships and battlecruisers**: 6 new H-class battleships, 4 existing (Bismarck, Tirpitz, Scharnhorst, Gneisenau), 3 O-class battlecruisers
- **15 Panzerschiffe**: Armored cruisers (Deutschland-class type)
- **4 aircraft carriers**: Graf Zeppelin class
- **23 cruisers**: Various classes
- **22 Spähkreuzer**: Large destroyers
- **~249 U-boats**: Submarine force expansion
- **Total: ~800 ships** including smaller vessels

Construction scheduled 1939-1946, requiring 201,000 personnel and 33 billion Reichsmark. The war's outbreak in September 1939 halted Plan Z; incomplete ships were scrapped for submarine construction materials.

### Surface Fleet Strategy

Raeder's strategic doctrine prioritized **surface fleet power** over submarine warfare:

**Commerce Raiding Philosophy**: Deploy powerful surface raiders (pocket battleships, heavy cruisers) to disrupt British supply lines across the Atlantic and Indian Oceans.

**Fleet-in-Being Strategy**: Use the threat of the High Seas Fleet to tie down British naval resources, forcing the Royal Navy to maintain overwhelming superiority in home waters.

**Notable Operations Under His Command**:
- **Operation Weserübung** (Norway, 1940): Naval invasion that secured iron ore supply routes
- **Bismarck Sortie** (1941): Despite the battleship's loss, demonstrated the threat potential of German surface forces
- **Channel Dash** (1942): Escape of Scharnhorst, Gneisenau, and Prinz Eugen through the English Channel

### Naval Construction

Raeder oversaw the design and construction of formidable warships:

**Engineering Accomplishments**:
- Bismarck-class battleships: Among the most powerful battleships of the era
- Deutschland-class (Panzerschiffe): Diesel-powered armored cruisers
- Type XXI U-boats: Advanced submarine design (completed after his tenure)

**Logistics and Infrastructure**:
- Expanded shipyard capacity across Germany
- Developed synthetic fuel programs for naval operations
- Built extensive naval base infrastructure in occupied territories

### Leadership Philosophy

**Professional Naval Officer**: Career spanning both World Wars, deeply traditional in naval doctrine

**Strategic Vision**: Believed Germany needed 15-20 years of peace to build a navy capable of challenging Britain

**Nazi Collaboration**: Loyal supporter of Hitler's expansionist policies in the 1930s. Initiated planning for Norway invasion independently. Transmitted war crimes orders without objection. Grew frustrated with strategic interference only after repeated setbacks, not due to moral objections.

## Nazi Collaboration and War Crimes

### Rearmament in Violation of Versailles Treaty

Raeder commanded the German Navy from 1928, systematically violating the Treaty of Versailles before Hitler took power. He later testified that violations were "a matter of honour for every man." The German Admiralty deceived foreign governments, Germany's own legislature, and at times its own government about secret rearmament including submarine construction, electronic warfare development, and covert training programs abroad.

### Planning Aggressive War

Raeder actively participated in planning Nazi Germany's wars of aggression:
- Attended the Hoszbach Conference (November 1937) where Hitler outlined plans for territorial expansion
- Received war directives for invasions of Austria, Czechoslovakia, Poland, and Yugoslavia
- **Norway Invasion (1940)**: The Nuremberg Tribunal found "the conception of the invasion of Norway first arose in the mind of Raeder" rather than Hitler. Raeder initiated planning in October 1939 and later called it his "great contribution to the war"

### War Crimes

**Commando Order (1942)**:
In October 1942, Hitler ordered all captured Allied commandos executed without trial, even if in uniform or attempting surrender. **Raeder transmitted this order through the Kriegsmarine without objection** and ordered the execution of captured British Royal Marines after Operation Frankton (December 1942). Under oath at Nuremberg, Raeder testified he believed the order was "justified" and the executions were "no war crime." The International Military Tribunal disagreed, finding the Commando Order a direct breach of the laws of war.

**Unrestricted Submarine Warfare**:
Raeder authorized submarine attacks on merchant vessels of neutral nations and machine-gunning of survivors, violating the 1936 London Naval Protocol.

### Nuremberg Conviction

On October 1, 1946, the International Military Tribunal found Raeder **guilty on all three counts**:
- Count 1: Conspiracy to wage aggressive war
- Count 2: Crimes against peace
- Count 3: War crimes

Raeder was sentenced to life imprisonment. The tribunal noted he "absorbed the aggressive war charges more fully than any of the other defendants." He expected execution and was "deeply shocked" by life imprisonment. Released September 26, 1955 after serving 9 years (ostensibly for ill health, actually due to Cold War politics and West Germany joining NATO). **Raeder died in 1960, unrepentant and viewing himself as a professional officer who had done his duty.**

## Modern Applications

### Design & Architecture Excellence

Raeder's approach to **long-term strategic planning** and **infrastructure development** offers lessons for modern engineering, though his work must be separated from its criminal context:

**System Architecture**: Plan for scale before you need it. Raeder's Plan Z was designed for a 10-year buildup - modern systems should similarly plan for future capacity.

**Resource Allocation**: Balance between immediate needs and long-term capability. His constant battle for resources mirrors modern trade-offs between technical debt and new features.

**Quality Over Quantity**: Raeder prioritized fewer, more capable ships (Bismarck) over mass production - the "build it right" philosophy.

**Ethical Consideration**: Technical excellence without moral judgment enables atrocities. Raeder's case demonstrates that professional competence must be paired with ethical responsibility.

### Project Management

**Construction Pipeline Management**: Raeder coordinated simultaneous construction of dozens of capital ships across multiple shipyards - modern equivalent of managing complex multi-team software projects.

**Risk Management**: His warnings about premature war demonstrate understanding that complex systems require adequate development time.

## Historical Context

Raeder served as Commander-in-Chief of the German Navy for 15 years (1928-1943), spanning the Weimar Republic and Nazi era. He systematically violated the Versailles Treaty before Hitler's rise to power and became an active participant in planning wars of aggression. His transmission of the Commando Order and authorization of unrestricted submarine warfare constituted direct war crimes.

At Nuremberg, he was convicted on all charges and sentenced to life imprisonment, though released in 1955 for political reasons. He never apologized or acknowledged wrongdoing, maintaining until his death in 1960 that he was simply a professional officer following orders.

**Legacy**: Raeder's technical competence in naval architecture and long-term planning is overshadowed by his willing collaboration with the Nazi regime and direct responsibility for war crimes. His case demonstrates that professional expertise does not excuse participation in criminal enterprises.

---

## Historical Controversies

**Cold War Narratives**: Some Cold War-era German sources minimize Raeder's war crimes or portray him as having significant conflicts with Hitler. Post-reunification scholarship with access to East German archives presents a more critical assessment of his active collaboration with Nazi expansionism.

**"Professional Officer" Defense**: Raeder maintained he was apolitical and simply following orders. The Nuremberg Tribunal rejected this defense, noting his enthusiastic participation in planning aggressive wars and transmitting criminal orders.
