### 2. Dowding's Official Despatch (London Gazette, Sept 10-11, 1946)

**Citation**: The London Gazette, Issue 37719, Supplement, pp. 4543-4571

**URL**: https://www.ibiblio.org/hyperwar/UN/UK/LondonGazette/37719.pdf

**Access level**: FULL - Complete 30-page PDF downloaded and read

**Read status**: Complete reading (4,238 lines extracted text, 30 PDF pages)

**Document details**:
- Submitted: August 20, 1941 (by Dowding to Secretary of State for Air)
- Published: September 10-11, 1946 (5 years delay)
- Scope: Complete operational account of Battle of Britain (July 10 - Oct 31, 1940)

**Reading time**: ~2 hours (systematic reading, paragraph-by-paragraph with evidence extraction)

#### Key Evidence Extracted (with paragraph numbers)

**1. France Fighter Decision - Strategic Restraint**

**Para 15-16** - Pre-war fighter shortage:
> "Our output at the beginning of the war was about 2 Hurricanes and 2 Spitfires per diem... I therefore regarded with some apprehension the general policy of sending Home Defence Fighter Units to France; but, as it was clear that such an attitude was politically untenable, I wrote on the i6th September, 1939, a letter to the Air Ministry."

**Para 30-35** - Escalating French demands (May 1940):
- Initial: 6 squadrons in France (April 1940)
- +4 complete squadrons when fighting began
- +32 pilots/aircraft (equivalent 2 squadrons, May 13)
- +8 half-squadrons immediately after
- **Total: equivalent 16 squadrons lost**
- Plus 4 squadrons fighting daily, returning to England nightly

**Para 38** - Breaking point:
> "When the Dunkerque evacuation was complete I had only 3 Day-Fighting Squadrons which had not been engaged in Continental fighting, and 12 Squadrons were in the line for the second time after having been withdrawn to rest and re-form."

**Para 40-41** - War Cabinet appearance (May 1940):
> "I sought and obtained permission to appear in person before the War Cabinet and to state my case. I was accorded a courteous and sympathetic hearing, and to my inexpressible relief my arguments prevailed and it was decided to send no more Fighter Reinforcements to France except to cover the final evacuation.
>
> I know what it must have cost the Cabinet to reach this decision, but I am profoundly convinced that this was one of the great turning points of the war."

**Assessment**: Dowding explicitly credits War Cabinet decision, NOT his own genius. Demonstrates restraint in self-presentation.

**2. Beaverbrook and Production Crisis**

**Para 42**:
> "Another decision, of perhaps equal importance, was taken at about this time. I refer to the appointment of Lord Beaverbrook to the post of Minister of Aircraft Production. The effect of this appointment can only be described as magical, and thereafter the Supply situation improved to such a degree that the heavy aircraft wastage which was later incurred during the 'Battle of Britain' ceased to be the primary danger, its place being taken by the difficulty of producing trained fighter pilots in adequate numbers."

**Assessment**: Dowding credits Beaverbrook with solving production crisis. No claim of personal contribution to production solutions.

**3. Radar/Radio-Location System - Distributed Credit**

**Para 67** - System operations credit:
> "The credit for working out this system in conjunction with the Home Office is due largely to Air Vice-Marshal A. D. Cunningham, C.B.E."

**Para 73** - Filter Room credit:
> "The credit for working out the complicated details of the Filter Room belongs largely to Wing Commander (now Group Captain) R. G. Hart, C.B.E."

**Para 59** - Observer Corps credit:
> "The credit for building up and developing the Observer Corps in recent years is due largely to its Commandant, Air Commodore A. D. Warrington Morris; C.M.G., O.B.E."

**Assessment**: Dowding systematically credits subordinates for system development. No claim of personal innovation.

**4. Park and Tactical Innovation**

**Para 128-129**:
> "I would like to pay tribute to the foresight of Air Vice-Marshal K. R. Park, C.B., M.C., D.F.C., for the way in which he adjusted his tactics and interception methods to meet each new development as it occurred.
>
> Tactical control was, as has already been stated, devolved to the Groups; but tactical methods were normally laid down by Command Headquarters. During periods of intense fighting, however, there was no time for consultation, and Air Vice-Marshal Park acted from day to day on his own initiative. We discussed matters as opportunity offered."

**Para 202** - Park's tactical report (Dowding quotes extensively):
> "The general plan adopted was to engage the enemy high-fighter screen with pairs of Spitfire Squadrons from Hornchurch and Biggin Hill half-way between London and the coast, and so enable Hurricane Squadrons from London Sectors to attack bomber formations..."

**Assessment**: Dowding explicitly credits Park with tactical innovation and autonomous decision-making during crisis. Quotes Park's own report extensively.

**5. Big Wing Controversy**

**Para 198-201** - Dowding's position:
> "During this third phase the problem arose, in an acute form, of the strength of Fighter formations which we should employ... two squadrons were generally used by A.V.-M. Park in No. ii Group. He had the responsibility of meeting attacks as far to the Eastward as possible, and the building up of a four-squadron formation involved the use of a rendezvous for aircraft from two or more aerodromes. This led to delay and lack of flexibility in leadership.
>
> ...I may preface my remarks by stating that I am personally in favour of using Fighter formations in the greatest strength of which circumstances will permit, and, in the Dunkerque fighting, where we could choose our time and build up our formations on the outward journey, I habitually employed four-Squadron formations..."

**Para 203-204** - Big Wing failure data:
> "I think that, if the policy of big formations had been attempted at this time in No. 11 Group, many more Bombers would have reached their objectives without opposition.
>
> Air Vice-Marshal Park also quotes the results of the ten large formations ordered from Duxford into No. u Group in the last half of October, when the Germans were employing Fighter-types only. Nine of these sorties made no interception, and the tenth destroyed one Me. 109."

**Assessment**: Dowding provides DATA on Big Wing effectiveness: 9/10 sorties made NO interception. This is empirical evidence, not opinion.

**6. Overclaiming and Intelligence Limitations**

**Para 113-115** - Casualty estimates:
> "I must disclaim any exact accuracy in the above figures... are put forward as an honest approximation. Judging by results, they are perhaps not far out.
>
> The German claims were, of course, ludicrous; they may have been deceived about our casualties, but they know they were lying about their own.
>
> I remember being cross-examined in August by the Secretary of State for Air about the discrepancy. He was anxious about the effect on the American people of the wide divergence between the claims of the two sides. I replied that the Americans would soon find out the truth; if the Germans' figures were accurate they would be in London in a week, otherwise they would not."

**Para 116** - Radio-Location limitations:
> "Our estimate of German casualties, then, may be taken as reasonably accurate for practical purposes; but our estimates of the strength in which attacks were made is based on much less reliable evidence. The Radio-Location system could give only a very approximate estimate of numbers and was sometimes in error by three or four hundred per cent. This is no reflection on the System, which was not designed or intended to be accurate in the estimation of considerable numbers..."

**Para 117-120** - Counting difficulties:
> "Even the numerical estimates made by pilots who encountered large formations in the air are likely to be guesswork in many instances. Opportunities for deliberate counting of enemy aircraft were the exception rather than the rule...
>
> My personal opinion is that, on days of slight activity, our estimates are reasonably accurate, but that they probably err on the low side on days of heavy fighting when many and large formations were employed."

**Assessment**: Dowding ADMITS uncertainty, acknowledges intelligence limitations, refuses to overclaim precision. Contrasts with German propaganda ("ludicrous").

**7. Critical Situation - Contradicts Hagiography**

**Para 106-107** (critiquing Air Ministry propaganda pamphlet):
> "The second point is of greater importance. I quote from page 33: 'What the Luftwaffe failed to do was to destroy the Fighter Squadrons of the Royal Air Force, which were, indeed, stronger at the end of the battle than at the beginning.' (The italics are mine.)
>
> This statement, even if intended only for popular consumption, tends to lead to an attitude of complacency which may be very dangerous in the future. Whatever the study of paper returns may have shown, the fact is that the situation was critical in the extreme."

**Para 193-195** - Deteriorating situation (late August):
> "The effective strength of the Command was running down, though the fact was not known to the public, nor, I hoped, to the Germans... Our own pilots were fighting with unabated gallantry and determination.
>
> The confidence of the German High Command probably received something of a shock about this time. The sustained resistance which they were meeting in South-East England probably led them to believe that Fighter Squadrons had been withdrawn, wholly or in part, from the North in order to meet the attack."

**Para 196-197** - Pilot exhaustion:
> "Our own pilots were fighting with the utmost gallantry and determination, but the mass raids on London, which were the main feature of the third phase of the Battle, involved a tremendous strain on units which could no longer be relieved as such. Some Squadrons were flying 50 and 60 hours per diem.
>
> Many of the pilots were getting very tired. An order was in existence that all pilots should have 24 hours' leave every week..."

**Assessment**: Dowding CONTRADICTS official propaganda narrative of strength at battle's end. Admits critical situation, exhaustion, declining effectiveness.

#### Reliability Assessment

**Tier**: **Tier 1** (official military document)

**Characteristics**:
1. **Under oath/official capacity**: Submitted to Secretary of State for Air, published in official government gazette
2. **Contradicts self-interest**: Admits intelligence failures, overclaiming uncertainty, critical weakness, credits others extensively
3. **Empirical data**: Provides specific numbers for Big Wing failures (9/10 no interception)
4. **Self-critical tone**: Corrects Air Ministry propaganda, admits limitations
5. **Distributed credit**: Systematically attributes successes to subordinates (Cunningham, Hart, Park, Beaverbrook, War Cabinet)

**Comparison to hagiographic claims**:
- Hagiography: "Dowding's System"
- Dowding (para 67, 73): Credits Cunningham and Hart
- Hagiography: "Dowding saved Britain"
- Dowding (para 41): Credits War Cabinet decision
- Hagiography: "Dowding was tactical genius"
- Dowding (para 129): Credits Park's autonomous innovation
- Hagiography: "Fighter Command stronger at battle's end"
- Dowding (para 107): "This statement... is wrong... the situation was critical in the extreme"

**Limitations**:
- Written August 1941 (10 months after battle) - memory limitations possible
- Published Sept 1946 (5 years delay) - potential political editing, though tone suggests minimal interference
- Does not have access to German records (written before 1947 Hansard revelation)
- Cannot verify all operational details independently

**Time spent reading**: ~2 hours (complete systematic reading with evidence extraction)
