## Critical Correction: Shell Shock Understanding

### Current Profile Statement (OVERSIMPLIFIED)
> "Patton didn't believe shell shock existed"

### Primary Source Evidence
**August 6, 1943 diary**:
> "saw two men completely out from shell shock. One kept going through the motions of crawling. The doctor told me they were going to give them an injection to put them to sleep and that probably they would wake up alright."

**December 30, 1944 letter to Hughes**:
> "Commanders are being directed to require an examination by a medical officer in all cases involving the relief of combat officers as the result of combat exhaustion... the officer will of course be disposed of through medical channels or given the proper treatment including a leave if that appears desirable."

**Apology statements (post-slapping)**:
> "always regarded cases of 'shell shock' as being most tragic"

### Recommended Revision

**Replace**: "Patton didn't believe shell shock existed"

**With**:
"Patton recognized shell shock as a legitimate medical condition but adhered to an outdated World War I definition that required complete incapacitation (paralysis, total loss of control). When he encountered Private Kuhl and Private Bennett in August 1943, both soldiers were coherent enough to explain their condition, leading Patton to conclude they were malingerers using hospitals to escape combat rather than genuinely shell-shocked. His August 6, 1943 diary shows he recognized other soldiers as 'completely out from shell shock,' and he later directed commanders to provide medical evaluation and treatment for combat-exhausted officers. The slapping incidents reveal not a blanket denial of combat stress, but an obsolete understanding of its symptoms combined with extreme intolerance for perceived cowardice."

**Impact**: Adds psychological complexity and historical accuracy. Shows Patton's failure was not ignorance but rigidity in applying outdated diagnostic criteria.
