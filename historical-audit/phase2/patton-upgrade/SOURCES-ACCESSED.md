**Source**: https://tile.loc.gov/storage-services/service/gdc/gdccrowd/mss/mss35634/001/10/00110.txt
**Access level**: FULL TEXT (transcribed)
**Read**: COMPLETE for August 1944 sections

**Key evidence extracted**:

**Assessment**: SELF-GENERATED PROPAGANDA. Patton made this claim himself, contemporaneously, in his private diary. This is Tier 1 evidence (primary source, contemporary, unfiltered).

**Reliability**: Highest (subject's own words, written at the time)

**Limitations**: None - full access to transcribed text.

#### Diary Volume: August 26, 1943 - February 8, 1944

**Source**: https://tile.loc.gov/storage-services/service/gdc/gdccrowd/mss/mss35634/003/04/00304.txt
**Access level**: FULL TEXT (transcribed)
**Read**: COMPLETE for December 1943 sections (slapping incident references)

**Key evidence extracted**:

**December 1, 1943 entry** (retrospective reference to August incidents):
> "I had been dealing with heroes. I saw two men whom I thought were cowards. Naturally I was not too gentle with them. This is exactly true, but there is no use repeating it."

**December 7, 1943**: Discussed incident with Under Secretary of War McCloy

**December 8, 1943**: Harry Hopkins told Patton "the incident was closed"

**December 25, 1943**: Expressed anger at Drew Pearson for publicizing incidents: "I will live to see him die"

**Assessment**: Patton's diary treats the slapping incidents obliquely, referring to them as "the Incident" rather than detailed accounts. He exhibits restraint in diary entries, suggesting awareness of historical scrutiny.

**CRITICAL FINDING**: The quote "gave him the devil, slapped his face with my gloves and kicked him out of the hospital" does NOT appear in Patton's diary. Pilot research attributed this to Patton's diary but it appears to originate from Ladislas Farago's biography, not primary diary text.

**Reliability**: Highest for what Patton wrote; note absence of detailed account.

**Limitations**: August 1943 entries not yet accessed (may contain contemporaneous account). Current access is retrospective December entries only.

#### Diary Volume: December 1944 (Battle of the Bulge)

**Source**: https://tile.loc.gov/storage-services/service/gdc/gdccrowd/mss/mss35634/003/09/00309.txt
**Access level**: FULL TEXT (transcribed)
**Read**: COMPLETE for December 1944 Battle of the Bulge sections

**Key evidence extracted**:

**December 18, 1944** (initial briefing):
- Patton stated he could attack with three divisions by December 22
- Proposed 4th Armored, 26th, and 80th divisions
- Bradley "seemed satisfied" with timeline

**Operational execution**:
- Coordinated movements "by telephone through General Gay and a fine staff at Nancy"
- Accomplished mobilization "in less than twelve hours" what "typically required a week"

**December 26, 1944** (Bastogne relief):
> "At 1845 they made contact, and Bastogne was liberated."

**Assessment**: Confirms 72-hour pivot claim as accurate. The timeline from December 19 (Verdun conference) to December 22 (attack launch) is approximately 72 hours. Full relief achieved December 26 (7 days total).

**Reliability**: Highest (contemporary operational account)

**Limitations**: None - full access to Battle of the Bulge period.

**Access level**: FULL TEXT (transcribed)

**March 23, 1945**: References "crossing of the Rhine" when dating casualty statistics forward.

**Reliability**: Highest (contemporary account)

**Limitations**: The actual moment of crossing and immediate personal reflections not included in this document section.

## Tier 2: Modern Scholarship - FULL READING

### 2. Harry Yeide: "(W)Archives: The Myth of Germany's Obsession with Patton"

**Source**: War on the Rocks (August 29, 2014)
**URL**: https://warontherocks.com/2014/08/warchives-the-myth-of-germanys-obsession-with-patton/
**Access level**: FULL TEXT (open access)
**Read**: COMPLETE

**Methodology**: Yeide accessed German primary sources (Army Group B records, military intelligence documents)

**Key findings**:

1. **German awareness timeline**:
   - NO mention of Patton in German records when he took II Corps command (March 1943)
   - First reference: mid-May 1943 (merely noting appointment to unit he'd already left)
   - Initial German assessment: "energetic and responsibility-loving command personality" (routine observation)

2. **Sicily campaign**: Germans unimpressed after Patton's Palermo advance (through already-abandoned territory)

3. **D-Day/FUSAG**: Germans identified Patton commanding FUSAG by mid-May 1944, but had already decided on Pas-de-Calais invasion months earlier

4. **Critical evidence**: "None of the surviving pre-invasion records from Army Group B mention Patton outside the FUSAG order of battle."

5. **Myth origin**: American leaders (particularly Marshall) ASSUMED Germans feared Patton without evidential support

**Assessment**: DEBUNKS "Germans feared Patton most" mythology. Based on German primary sources, this claim is post-war fabrication, not historical reality.

**Reliability**: Tier 1 (enemy primary sources analyzed by modern scholar)

**Limitations**: None - complete open-access article.

## Tier 3: Institutional Sources - PARTIAL/VERIFIED

### 3. Fort Driant Battle Statistics

**Sources**: Military History Fandom, historical accounts (aggregated)
**Access level**: FULL TEXT from multiple cross-referenced sources
**Read**: COMPLETE

**Verified statistics**:
- American killed: 64
- American wounded: 547
- American missing/captured: 187
- **Total American casualties**: 798 (some sources cite 734)
- Duration: September 27 assault → fort held until December 8, 1944
- Result: Patton's only lost battle in WWII

**Key contextual findings**:
- Intelligence failure: Patton "is bitter that his intelligence information has been wrong"
- Command responsibility: "Some people blamed Major General S. Leroy Irwin for the failure"
- Tactical reality: Air strikes (1,000 lb bombs) and 240mm artillery had "little effect" on fortifications

**Assessment**: Confirms pilot finding of significant failure inadequately documented in hagiographic accounts.

**Reliability**: Tier 2 (institutional sources, cross-verified)

**Limitations**: No access to complete after-action reports, relying on verified secondary sources.

### 4. Denazification Controversy Timeline

**Sources**: Military timelines, Richmond News Leader (October 3, 1945), military government documents
**Access level**: PARTIAL (secondary sources citing primary documents)
**Read**: COMPLETE for accessible material

**Verified timeline**:
- **September 22, 1945**: Patton told reporters he didn't see need for "this denazification thing"
- Compared Nazi Party membership to "Democratic and Republican election fight"
- **October 2, 1945**: Patton relieved of command for statements to press
- **October 3, 1945**: Richmond News Leader confirmed Patton "has been relieved of his command"
- Military government: "Replacement of Patton with new Third Army commander...in October 1945"

**Assessment**: Confirms pilot finding that denazification comments directly led to relief from command. This was not "controversial" — it was disqualifying failure of political judgment.

**Reliability**: Tier 2 (contemporary newspaper reporting, official timelines)

**Limitations**: Have not accessed Eisenhower's direct confrontation records or Patton's unmailed letter to Stimson.
