### 1.2 The Area Bombing Directive (February 14, 1942)

**Designation**: General Directive No. 5 (S.46368/111. D.C.A.S), formally amending General Directive No. 4
**Issuing authority**: Air Ministry, under Chief of Air Staff Sir Charles Portal
**Date**: February 14, 1942 — four days before Harris assumed command of Bomber Command

**Key language confirmed**:
> Bombing was to be "focused on the morale of the enemy civil population and in particular of the industrial workers."

**Portal's February 15, 1942 clarification** (the day after the directive):
Portal clarified that "the aiming points will be the built up areas, and not, for instance, the dockyards or aircraft factories."

Source: Bomber Command Museum Archives (Canada); military-history.fandom.com wiki. (SUMMARY — not original National Archives document)

**Significance**: This directive was issued BEFORE Harris took command. Harris did not create area bombing doctrine; he inherited it as policy. However, Harris was ideologically aligned with and enthusiastically implemented it. He was not merely an executor — he was a true believer who lobbied for exactly this approach.

**Limitation**: Original document text from National Archives not accessed. Characterizations confirmed across multiple secondary sources. UK National Archives AIR series contains original (AIR 14/3507 area), but not accessed in this research.

### 1.3 Churchill-Harris Relationship — Primary Documentation

**Churchill's original draft memo, March 28, 1945** (to Chiefs of Staff Committee):
> "It seems to me that the moment has come when the question of bombing of German cities simply for the sake of increasing the terror, though under other pretexts, should be reviewed. Otherwise we shall come into control of an utterly ruined land...We must see to it that our attacks do not do more harm to ourselves in the long run than they do to the enemy's immediate war effort. Pray let me have your views."

**Portal's revised draft** (replacing Churchill's original, which Churchill then signed):
> "It seems to me that the moment has come when the question of the so called 'area bombing' of German cities should be reviewed from the point of view of our own interests. If we come into control of an entirely ruined land, there will be a great shortage of accommodation for ourselves and our Allies; and we shall be unable to get housing materials out of Germany for our own needs because some temporary provision would have to be made for the Germans themselves."

**What happened**: Harris was "outraged" by Churchill's original memo. Portal persuaded Churchill to moderate it. Churchill signed Portal's sanitized version. The original draft with "terror" language was withdrawn.

Source: Martin Gilbert, *Road to Victory 1941–1945*, page 1257 (cited in WebSearch results). PAYWALLED — not directly accessed.

**Churchill's May 15, 1945 statement (public, before his distancing)**:
> "I believe that the massive achievement of Bomber Command will long be remembered as an example of duty nobly done."

**Churchill's contemporaneous wartime letter to Harris (1945)**:
> "All your operations were planned with great care and skill. They were executed in the face of desperate opposition and appalling hazards, they made a decisive contribution to Germany's final defeat. The conduct of the operations demonstrated the fiery gallant spirit which animated your aircrews."

**Churchill's January 6, 1946 letter to Attlee (on Harris recognition)**:
> "No C-in-C in the RAF after Lord Dowding bore so heavy a burden as he and none contributed more distinguished qualities."

Source: Martin Gilbert biography; International Churchill Society; Martin Gilbert blog. (PAYWALLED — full text not accessed; confirmed via multiple secondary citations)

**Churchill's omission of Dresden from his memoirs**: Confirmed by Sciences Po Knapp article (FULL ACCESS).

**Analysis of Churchill's conduct**: Churchill authorized area bombing; endorsed Harris in writing; then drafted a memo describing it as "terror...under other pretexts"; withdrew that memo; maintained public support until victory; then omitted the campaign from his victory speech and his memoirs. This is a documented pattern of political self-distancing after endorsing the policy. The sciences Po Knapp article describes this as strategic repositioning "to maintain plausible deniability."

### 1.4 Dresden — The Definitive Accounting

**German Historical Commission (Historikerkommission) 2010**:
- Established 2008 by city of Dresden
- Completed report 2010
- Finding: "at least 18,000 and at most 25,000" deaths (commission member Widera, Deutsche Welle)
- Alternative citation: "approximately 25,000 persons died in Dresden and another 30,000 were injured" (Warfare History Network)

**Sources confirming figure**:
- US Air Force Historical Support Division: "approximately 25,000 lost their lives"
- CBC News (March 18, 2010): Figures "significantly fewer" than previously estimated
- History.com: "22,700 to 25,000 dead" (2010)
- Deutsche Welle: "at least 18,000 and at most 25,000"
- Britannica: "25,000–35,000 civilians" (slightly higher range)

**Confirmed range**: 18,000–35,000 depending on source, with German Historical Commission establishing 18,000–25,000 as the scholarly consensus.

**Nazi propaganda figure**: Goebbels claimed 200,000+. David Irving's 1963 book *The Destruction of Dresden* circulated 150,000–200,000. Both are now thoroughly discredited by the 2010 Commission.

**Decision-making chain for Dresden**:
- Soviet General Aleksei Antonov requested Allied aerial support at Yalta Conference
- Churchill and Roosevelt committed to continued bombing at Yalta
- Joint Chiefs authorized attacks on German cities and infrastructure
- RAF Bomber Command (Harris) and US Eighth Air Force both executed
- Official RAF memo on the night of the attack described Dresden as "seventh largest city in Germany" and cited rail hub and military functions

**Military value claimed**:
- Rail hub with lines to Berlin, Prague, Vienna, Munich, Breslau, Leipzig, Hamburg (confirmed)
- Wehrmacht headquarters relocated there (claimed)
- 127 factories manufacturing rifles, machine guns, artillery, aircraft components, optical devices, and poison gas (claimed — sourced from Warfare History Network; independently verifiable?)
- Ammunition depots and military hospitals (claimed)

**Victor Gregg (British POW in Dresden)**:
> "Dresden had no defenses, no antiaircraft guns, no searchlights, nothing." (Warfare History Network, FULL ACCESS)

**Significance**: The POW witness statement directly contradicts the "military target" justification. An undefended city cannot be defended as a military objective under any reasonable interpretation of the laws of war.

**Date context**: Germany's defeat was certain by February 1945 (Red Army at Oder River, less than 100 miles from Berlin); not complete (Wehrmacht still fighting). This is the honest framing.

**Harris's stated position on Dresden**: Deflected responsibility upward ("much more important people"). Did NOT claim it was wrong. Did NOT express regret. Did claim it was militarily justified.

### 1.5 USSBS Findings on Area Bombing Effectiveness

**Source**: US Strategic Bombing Survey (USSBS), Summary Report (European War)
**Access level**: FULL — via anesi.com hosted text
**Reliability tier**: Tier 1 (primary contemporary official government investigation)

**Key findings on German morale**:
> "The morale of the German people deteriorated under aerial attack. The night raids were feared far more than daylight raids." (Page 4)

> "If they had been at liberty to vote themselves out of the war, they would have done so well before the final surrender." (Page 4)

BUT: "Workers continued production despite dissatisfaction." Morale "dented but not broken" — Germans under Nazi control lacked means to express dissent.

**Key findings on industrial production**:
- German war production continued INCREASING through 1944 despite attacks
- Economy was "substantially undermobilized" throughout the war
- "Plant machinery showed remarkable durability even when buildings were destroyed"
- Production "recovered substantially within a relatively few weeks" (Pages 3–4)

**Area bombing effectiveness ranking (CRITICAL FINDING)**:
- Area bombing: "Did not substantially affect the course of German war production" prior to autumn 1944 (Page 3)
- Oil campaign (USSBS finding): Most decisive — consumption exceeded production from May 1944; Speer reported fuel shortage reached "catastrophic proportions" by December 1944 (Page 9)
- Transportation: "The decisive blow that completely disorganized the German economy" — freight loadings collapsed from 900,000 cars to 214,000 by March (Pages 12–13)
- German leaders themselves: "Far more concerned over attacks on oil, chemical, steel, power, or transportation than on city areas" (Page 16)

**On Hamburg (USSBS)**:
- "60,000 to 100,000 people killed" (Hamburg figure in USSBS is higher than profile's "42,600+")
- "No subsequent city raid shook Germany as did that on Hamburg"
- German leaders considered further similar attacks potentially war-ending — yet industrial output continued rising
- RAF subsequently "returned with part of its force to attack on industrial targets" in autumn 1944 with "notably successful" results

**Overall verdict on strategic bombing**:
> "Allied air power was decisive in the war in Western Europe." (Page 16)

BUT this required "full domination of the air" and worked within a ground invasion strategy. Bombing alone did not force surrender.

**CRITICAL IMPLICATION FOR HARRIS**: The USSBS — a Tier 1 primary source — explicitly finds that area bombing "did not substantially affect German war production" before late 1944, while oil and transportation attacks were "far more" effective by German testimony. Harris's refusal to prioritize oil/transport over city bombing (area bombing) in 1943–44 was therefore strategically wrong by the evidence.

### 1.6 Albert Speer Testimony on Bombing Effectiveness

**Source**: USSBS interrogation of Albert Speer; secondary in World History Encyclopedia, Air University sources
**Access**: SUMMARY (interrogation document identified at digitalcommons.unl.edu but not fully accessed)

**On oil attacks (Speer to Hitler, 1944)**:
> "The enemy has struck us at one of our weakest points. If they persist at it this time, we will soon no longer have any fuel production worth mentioning."

**On aviation fuel specifically**:
> "The enemy has succeeded in increasing our losses of aviation gasoline up to 90 percent by June."

**On transportation attacks**:
Transportation cuts cut Germany off from essential coal supplies. Coal reserves fell from 18 days to 4.5 days (October 1944 to February 1945). Rail cars carrying coal fell from 21,000/day to 9,000/day.

**On strategic air campaign overall**:
> "As far as I can judge from the accounts I have read, no one has seen that this was the greatest lost battle on the Germans' side."

**On RAF city bombing specifically**:
Hamburg bombing "put the fear of god in me" (Speer, per NWWII Museum). But Hamburg production recovered to 80% within five months.

**Implication**: Speer's own testimony confirms oil/transport attacks were decisive; city area bombing caused fear and damage but was recovered from. Harris's doctrine was demonstrably less effective than the alternatives he resisted.

### 1.7 The Butt Report (August 1941)

**Access**: SUMMARY via multiple secondary sources
**Reliability**: Tier 1 (government commissioned report — primary document)

**What it found**:
- Of RAF aircraft dispatched, "only one in three got within 5 miles of their targets in Germany" (some sources: "one in five")
- Commissioned by David Bensusan-Butt under Churchill aide Frederick Lindemann
- Based on analysis of 100 raids on 28 different targets

**Churchill's response**:
> "This is a very serious paper and seems to require urgent attention." (Churchill, per multiple sources)

**Impact**: Triggered the shift to area bombing — because precision bombing was demonstrably not achievable with 1941 technology (inadequate navigation, no Pathfinders, no H2S radar).

**Critical time-bound analysis**: The Butt Report DOES justify the shift to area bombing in 1941-42, when precision was genuinely impossible. However, by 1944, Pathfinder Force navigation improvements, H2S radar, and OBOE blind bombing system substantially improved precision capability. The "precision was impossible" argument was valid in 1941; it was increasingly invalid by 1943-44 when Harris continued to prioritize city bombing.

### 1.9 Post-War Recognition — The Snub

**Timeline of events (verified)**:

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| May 13, 1945 | Churchill's victory speech — omits Bomber Command |
| May 15, 1945 | Churchill: "massive achievement of Bomber Command... duty nobly done" |
| July 26, 1945 | Labour Government (Attlee) takes power |
| August 1945 | No Bomber Command campaign medal announced |
| January 6, 1946 | Churchill writes to Attlee supporting Harris peerage; Attlee refuses |
| 1946 | Harris made Marshal of the Royal Air Force — consolation promotion |
| 1947 | *Bomber Offensive* published |
| 1951 | Churchill returns to power; offers Harris peerage; Harris refuses in protest |
| 1953 | Churchill insists; Harris accepts baronetcy (1st Baronet of Chipping Wycombe) |
| 2012–2013 | Bomber Command Memorial unveiled; Bomber Command Clasp finally awarded |

**Why denied**:
- Attlee's Labour government declined to forward Harris for peerage
- No specific Bomber Command campaign medal was created (crews received Air Crew Europe star and France/Germany stars)
- Sciences Po article (Knapp): Government actively distancing from deliberate civilian targeting that had become politically embarrassing
- Harris's own December 23, 1943 letter to Air Ministry warned that refusing acknowledgment "will inevitably lead to deplorable controversies"

**Harris on denial**: He refused the peerage when offered, as protest against the lack of recognition for his crews. He accepted the baronetcy (lower honor) in 1953 under Churchill's insistence.

**Political analysis**: The denial was not a legal verdict or moral condemnation. It was political self-protection — Churchill and Attlee both wanted to avoid association with deliberate targeting of civilian populations at a time when Nuremberg war crimes trials were ongoing and civilian immunity was being established in international law.

### 1.10 International Law — Was Area Bombing a War Crime?

**The Hague Rules of Aerial Warfare (1923)**:
- Drafted at an international conference
- Prohibited aerial bombardment for "terrorizing the civilian population" or targeting "non-military" property
- NEVER RATIFIED — not legally binding on any party in 1939–1945
- "The 1923 draft...was not ratified when hostilities broke out in Europe in 1939...never became legally binding"

Source: ICRC International Review; law.unimelb.edu.au paper (FULL ACCESS)

**The Hague Convention IV (1907)**:
- Does not address aerial warfare (aircraft barely existed in 1907)
- Covers land warfare bombardment; some attempted application to air bombing but no definitive ruling

**Nuremberg precedent on area bombing**:
> "Indiscriminate bombing charges were withdrawn at Nuremberg because the Allies' legal vulnerability on this issue was all too clear."

Source: Sciences Po Knapp article (FULL ACCESS) — this is a critical primary-level finding from an academic citing Nuremberg proceedings.

**Legal conclusion**: Area bombing was NOT illegal under international law as it stood in 1939–1945. The relevant rules had not been ratified. The Nuremberg tribunal implicitly acknowledged this by NOT prosecuting Allied bombing commanders. Harris was not a war criminal under the law of his time. This does not resolve the moral question.

**Moral status**: Under **just war theory** (which is not the same as international law), area bombing's deliberate targeting of civilians as a primary aim (not collateral damage) is harder to defend. The A.C. Grayling argument: area bombing "violated just war theory principles — neither necessary nor proportionate, and viable precision alternatives existed." However, "viable precision alternatives" in 1941–42 is contestable.

### 1.11 The Effectiveness Debate — Final Assessment

**The core question**: Did area bombing contribute to Germany's defeat?

**Yes (with qualifications)**:
- Forced Germany to divert ~10,000 anti-aircraft guns and hundreds of thousands of soldiers to home defense
- Destroyed Luftwaffe as operational force through attrition (though this was partly through air battles over Germany, not bombing itself)
- Created massive logistical burden on Germany (repair, evacuation, fire services)
- Speer himself: bombing opened "a second front long before the invasion of Europe"
- Air superiority achieved through bomber campaigns was essential for D-Day
- USSBS: "Allied air power was decisive in the war in Western Europe" (though this includes all strategic bombing, not just area bombing)

**No (with substantial evidence)**:
- German industrial production CONTINUED RISING until late 1944 despite area bombing (USSBS Tier 1 source)
- Hamburg production recovered 80% within five months (NWWII Museum)
- German civilian morale "dented but not broken" — Germans continued working (USSBS)
- Oil and transport attacks proved "far more decisive" than city bombing (USSBS, Speer testimony)
- Harris resisted the Oil Plan in 1944, calling it "another panacea" — when oil attacks were demonstrably more effective
- Quebec Conference removed civilian morale as a bombing objective in August 1943 — Harris continued city bombing without the mandate

**The specific Harris culpability on the Oil Plan**:
- Harris "did not support the transportation plan" initially (DTIC source)
- Harris resisted Oil Plan specifically as "another of the panaceas so frequently pressed on the air forces" (DTIC source)
- He was "won over" to the Transportation Plan for D-Day support
- His resistance to Oil in particular was a strategic error by the evidence of the USSBS and Speer

**Honest assessment**: Area bombing contributed to Allied victory but was NOT the decisive element. It killed hundreds of thousands of German civilians while industrial production continued and morale did not break. Oil and transport attacks were demonstrably more effective. Harris's doctrinal rigidity in resisting oil/transport attacks in 1943-44 represents a strategic failure, not just a moral controversy.

## PART 3: KEY FINDINGS SUMMARY

### Finding 1: Harris Explicitly Stated Civilian Killing as an "Intended Aim"

Harris wrote in *Bomber Offensive* (1947) that civilian deaths were "accepted and intended aims" of bombing policy, explicitly NOT "by-products of attempts to hit factories." This is not "moral blindness" or "moral absolutism" — this is stated intent. His own words confirm deliberate targeting of civilian lives and morale.

### Finding 2: Area Bombing Was Policy Before Harris, But Harris Was Its Architect and Ideologue

The February 14, 1942 Area Bombing Directive was issued four days before Harris took command. Harris did not invent area bombing policy. However, Harris was an ideological advocate who had lobbied for this approach, and he executed it with personal conviction well beyond "following orders." He resisted alternatives (oil, transport) even when evidence showed they were more effective.

### Finding 3: Churchill Authorized Area Bombing Then Distanced Himself — This Was Political, Not Moral

Churchill's March 28, 1945 original draft described bombing "simply for the sake of increasing the terror" — then withdrew it after Harris objected. Churchill praised Harris in writing, omitted Bomber Command from his victory speech, and omitted Dresden from his memoirs. His post-war distancing was political self-protection, not moral evolution.

### Finding 4: Area Bombing Did Not Break German Morale or Significantly Degrade Production Until Late 1944

USSBS (Tier 1 source): Area bombing "did not substantially affect German war production" before autumn 1944. German morale "dented but not broken." Oil and transport attacks were demonstrably more effective by German testimony (Speer) and USSBS findings. Harris's doctrinal rigidity in resisting oil/transport in 1943–44 was a strategic error supported by the evidence.

### Finding 5: Dresden Casualty Figures Have Been Systematically Inflated

German Historical Commission (2010): 18,000–25,000 deaths. Nazi propaganda originally circulated 200,000–500,000. David Irving's 1963 book perpetuated 150,000–200,000. The inflated figures were used both by Nazi propagandists and later by post-war critics of area bombing. Using inflated figures is empirically dishonest regardless of political position.

### Finding 6: Area Bombing Was NOT Illegal Under International Law (1939–1945)

The 1923 Hague Rules of Aerial Warfare were never ratified. Nuremberg prosecutors did not charge Allied bombing commanders — and explicitly withdrew such charges recognizing Allied legal vulnerability. Harris was not a war criminal under the law of his time. The moral question is separate from the legal question and is not resolved by the legal finding.

### Finding 7: Harris Refused Responsibility for Dresden by Deflecting Upward

Harris wrote in *Bomber Offensive*: "The attack on Dresden was at the time considered a military necessity by much more important people than myself." This is a deflection — "I was following orders" — that contradicts his broader self-portrayal as the strategic architect of area bombing. A man who claims credit for the strategic bombing campaign cannot simultaneously disclaim responsibility for Dresden.

### Finding 8: The Post-War Denial of Recognition Was Political, Not a Moral Verdict

Attlee denied Harris a peerage; no Bomber Command campaign medal issued until 2012–2013. This was not a legal judgment or moral condemnation — it was political self-distancing from civilian targeting at a time when Nuremberg trials were establishing civilian immunity in international law. Harris's own 1943 warning proved prescient: the lack of acknowledgment did indeed "lead to deplorable controversies."

### Finding 9: Harris's Sandhurst Acknowledgment (1975) — The Most Honest Statement

Harris himself said in 1975 that "Germany would have tried him as a war criminal had they won." This is the most honest assessment of his own position. He understood the moral ambiguity; he did not believe he was wrong, but he acknowledged the standards-dependent nature of the judgment.
